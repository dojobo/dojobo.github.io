A recent passion project in my free time has been contributing to Wikipedia. In doing some pleasure nonfiction reading about my home state, Louisiana, I found that there was scant coverage of some of the topics on Wikipedia. I think it’s important for my unique and special state to be well represented and to be known to the rest of the world. Wikipedia is a cornerstone of today’s information ecosystem, fulfilling a broad variety of information needs for millions of readers, for free, and acting as a portal for substantial Web traffic. Wikipedia’s importance is perhaps even greater in light of the ongoing pollution of search results by machine-generated nonsense.

Although I am not an expert on the topics which I’m writing about, I know enough about them to find relevant information, transfer it into Wikipedia, and cite the source. As an experienced librarian, I am also already familiar with threshold concepts like Authority Control, No Original Research, Notability, and Neutral Point of View, as well as the importance of citing claims. Having an understanding of knowledge graphs/Linked Data also helps in contributing to Wikidata.

Adapting public-domain works

I created the following articles by adapting works that are in the public domain, due to their production by a federal agency, the Environmental Protection Agency. There are many ecoregion articles on Wikipedia already, but these in particular, at least in their EPA formulation, were not yet represented. Although I can claim no credit for the writing, formatting the material as a Wikipedia article makes it much more readily accessible. (For comparison, the information is otherwise in PDFs of large posters and Word documents on the EPA’s website.) I also added value by linking to relevant existing articles and incorporating some images. The images were already available for use via Wikimedia Commons.

  • Southeastern Plains (ecoregion) (PDF)
  • Mississippi Valley Loess Plains (ecoregion) (PDF)
  • Southern Coastal Plain (ecoregion) (PDF)

By far what has been most exciting about this process has been creating maps in ArcGIS Pro, which I am recently learning for an unrelated project, to supplement the articles.
