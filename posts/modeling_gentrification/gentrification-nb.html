Predictively Modeling Gentrification in Pittsburgh

Dominic Bordelon

Gentrification is a socioeconomic phenomenon seen in many postindustrial American cities. It is a driver of displacement of marginalized individuals and communities from their longtime homes.

In our local context, we investigate the following questions:

  • Can we quantify and estimate gentrification locally using open (i.e., publicly available) data?
  • How can we characterize a "pre-gentrification" or emergent gentrification area; in particular, which variables are strong predictors of gentrification?
  • Can we predict where gentrification is likely to occur in Pittsburgh in the future?

Data sources: Western Pennsylvania Regional Data Center; US Census Bureau (see full listing in Section 7).

Context: This analysis was proposed by WPRDC to undergraduate students of Dr. Amin Rahimian's Data for Social Good course at the University of Pittsburgh in Fall 2023. I was invited to mentor the students working on this problem. I guided the team's investigation generally: literature review, methodological approach, surveying and preprocessing of data, modeling, visualization, and interpretation. Their results can be seen on the course GitHub repo. Since then, I have extended the investigation into the current analysis as a portfolio item.

1. Related work

We mimic the approach used by William Thackway, et al. (2023) to predictively model gentrification in Sydney, Australia. Their 80 predictor variables include age, country of birth, education level, employment status, income, occuptation, rental/ownership, housing prices and supply, number of food and leisure businesses, and others. Their model is a random forest regressor which predicts ranking in an inequality index, SEIFA, developed by the Australian Bureau of Statistics.

2. Methods

We utilize the publicly available Housing Market Value Analysis 2021, which is a unsupervised cluster analysis, labeling block groups in Allegheny County with an "MVA category" A through J. We select one of these categories, F, as an estimator of "emergent gentrification." While this data set is convenient, we should note that it is focused only on housing market values and types of housing markets within the region--the MVA is not itself concerned with gentrification nor would it capture social dimensions of gentrification.

The market value categories are as follows:

  • "A" markets have the highest housing values, experience the largest level of new construction, have the highest owner occupancy levels, and experience little housing distress (such as residential vacancy and foreclosure).
  • "B" markets have elevated housing values, experience substantial amounts of new construction, have more renters than owners, and experience little housing distress.
  • "C" markets have above average housing values, experience about average levels of new constructon, have the highest levels of owner occupancy, and experience little housing distress.
  • "D" markets have average housing values, experience half the countywide average amount of new construction, have more renters than owners, experience average levels of foreclosure, and have low levels of vacant lots and poor or worse condition properties.
  • "E" markets have slightly lower than average housing values, experience little new construcion, have high levels of owner occupancy, have above average amounts of vacant land, and about aveage levels of foreclosure.
  • "F" markets have slightly lower than average housing values, experience slightly above average amounts of new construction, have more owners than renters, and have high levels of renters with a subsidy.
  • "G" markets have below average housing values, experience little new construction, have more owners than renters, and experience above average levels of foreclosure and residential vacant land.
  • "H" markets have housing values well below the countywide average, exprience little new construction, have about even numbers of renters and owners, have the highest share of residential vacant land, and the highest levels of foreclosure.
  • "I" markets have the second lowest housing values, experience very little new construction, have the highest share of renters with a subsidy, experience the highest levels of building violations, and have elevated shares of poor or worse condition properties, vacant residential lot area, and foreclosure.
  • "J" markets have the lowest housing values (although there is a substantial amount of variability in those prices), more renters than homeowners, the highest share of poor or worse condition properties, and elevated shares of building violations, vacant lots, and foreclosure.

A-C are described as "Robust;" D-F are "Steady;" G and H are "Transitional," and I and J are "Stressed." We focus on F as the site of emergent or incipient gentrification because of lower than average housing values (prone to targeting), above average new construction (e.g., luxury condominiums), and high levels of renters with subsidy (more vulnerable to displacement than other classes of residents).

Most of the available Census and housing data is aggregated to the tract level. The MVA, however, labels market categories at the block group level (multiple block groups comprise a tract). We therefore normalize the MVA categories proportionally within each tract, using population. Each tract has ten new variables for the MVA categories, collectively summing to 1.0.

Another way we practice convenience sampling is that many of the WPRDC datasets begin in 2012 with yearly observations thereafter. Given that the 2021 MVA analysis was based on 2017-19 data, we thought it would be interesting to treat these MVA labels as "outcomes" of 2012 data. Therefore we fit a regressor which treats 2012 data as $X$ and ~2017 MVA conditions as $Y$. We utilize a regressor rather than a classifier since the MVA categories have been converted to proportional (i.e., numeric) dummy variables. We choose random forest as our modeling method because Thackway et al. (2023) use this method; because it is said to typically deliver good results; and because it can handle a multi-output $Y$ (one value for each proportional MVA dummy variable).

Once a model is fit, we will generate predictions using 2020 housing and Census data, representing MVA category composition at some future time. This will produce predicted MVA 10-category makeup for all tracts. Since we are interested only in category F, we will then caculate the predicted change in F proportion for each tract. Those tracts with a high F prediction or a high rate of growth prediction in F are at risk of gentrification. Given the large number of predictors, we will use a feature selection heuristic to somewhat simplify the model. We will assess the model's accuracy on both training and test (holdout) data, and then tune the model to reduce overfitting.

4. Fitting and tuning a random forest regression model

Following Thackway et al. (2023), we now implement a random forest regressor in order to predict future areas of gentrification. More specifically, the output or $Y$ of this model will be an array of ten numeric values, corresponding to the ten dummy variables created in Section 3.1.2; each dummy variable is a proportion of MVA class membership, A through J.

Prior to estimating the predictions, we must fit ("train") the model. To accomplish this, we treat the 2021 MVA categories (based on 2017-19 data) as outputs of 2012 data. The time lag between 2012 and 2017 accounts roughly for the passage of time. So in summary, in fitting the model, $Y$ will be 2021 MVA categories, and $X$ will be the many predictors we have chosen.

Once an initial model is fit, we will evaluate its performance according to accuracy and explore possible improvements.

We have very many predictors, not all of which may be useful for our model. Therefore, we will use sklearn.feature_selection.SelectFromModel() to select only the more relevant half of all the features.

The training data have an MSE of only 0.43%; since this is quite a bit lower than 2.98%, overfitting may be indicated. We can possibly improve the accuracy of the model by experimenting with the hyperparameters, and then choosing the model with similar MSE on unseen test data.

Another way to evaluate the model is according to its $R^2$ score. It answers the question, "how much of the model's output is explained by the inputs?" There are necessarily practical limits on $R^2$ (i.e., irreducible error) because of unaccounted-for factors, but we would like the model to have similar $R^2$ on both training and test sets.

4.3 Model tuning

We can adjust the model empirically by trying a variety of values among several hyperparameters (feature threshold, max tree depth, minimum samples per split, and minimum samples per leaf). To accomplish this, we'll make a matrix of hyperparameter combinations and then use grid search to iterate through possibilities. To save time on compute, we will randomize the grid search and try only 100 combinations within the hyperparameter space. Then we will select the best of those 100 models. (In fact, there will be many more models behind the scenes due to cross-validation; their results will be averaged.)

Although the RandomizedSearchCV includes a k-fold cross-validation splitter, I found through trial and error that $R^2$ was very low on test data, maxing at around 25%. Therefore, we will try retaining the test data until the end and testing only on the best model.

6. Discussion

Returning to our initial questions, we have preliminary quantification of gentrification using an housing market value category, informed by many variables. In the feature importance visualization, we see that, after median mortgage value, strong predictors of F are renter occupation, education of bachelor's or higher, median real estate taxes, and unemployed status.

With an $R^2$ of only 33% on the test data, one would not want to rely on the results of this mod for predicting that a particular area will or will not gentrifyel. However, there are many possible avenues for further exploration and potential improvement:

  • A wider array of data, such as presence of business types, and more demographic Census variables (e.g., occupation/industry)
  • Reducing the time gap between $X$ and $Y$ when fitting the model; ideally determining empirically an appropriate value.
  • Utilizing time series analysis and data from multiple years.
  • Taking into account distances (e.g., from downtown) and/or other forms of GIS modeling.
  • Experimenting with dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA) or deep learning methods.

This project has been a fascinating first step in exploring this questioanalytically n and becoming more familiar with model assessment and tuning.
